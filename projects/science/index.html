CORAL REEFS

Reefs are the most biodiverse ecosystems on the planet, and coral is a perfect example of why biodiversity is necessary. The coral itself is an animal, however, it wouldnâ€™t be what we think of without plant organisms called Zooxanthellae. They work in harmony with the coral photosynthesising to provide the coral with food. These tiny organisms are what gives coral its distinctive colors. The coral then uses this food to create calcium carbonate to form the beautiful structures we see on reefs. These are the structures that house tiny invertebrates which the fish feed on.

Reproduction in the reef is one of the most unique things about this ecosystem. It is believed by scientists that some small creatures are so rare that they may never meet another member of their species to reproduce in their whole lifetime. This means that very few organisms could be responsible for a whole generation of their species. Coral reproduces in an utterly remarkable way. On one day of the year, all the coral of one type will release packets of eggs and sperm. They will float to the surface to mix and then be taken by the tide to grow into new reefs over thousands of years.

Prey and predators live in harmony on reefs. Fish swim around aware of the threats around them, but safe in the knowledge that they could escape easily. The predators know this so they only attack when they will win in order to save energy.
